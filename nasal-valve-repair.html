Nasal Surgery

Understanding Nasal Valve Collapse

The nasal valve is the narrowest part of the nasal airway — the area just inside the nostril opening. It accounts for the greatest resistance to airflow in the entire respiratory tract. When the cartilage or soft tissue supporting the nasal valve is weak, it collapses inward during inhalation, dramatically reducing airflow.

Nasal valve repair addresses this weakness using proven techniques: spreader grafts widen the internal valve by spreading the upper lateral cartilages apart, while alar batten grafts reinforce the external valve against collapse. The LATERA absorbable implant provides an alternative for select patients who prefer to avoid cartilage grafting.

  • Spreader grafts and alar batten grafts — decades of proven outcomes
  • LATERA implant option for appropriate candidates
  • Purely functional — does not change the appearance of the nose
  • Can be combined with septoplasty, turbinate reduction, or FESS

When Nasal Valve Repair Is Recommended

Obstruction That Worsens With Breathing In

The hallmark of nasal valve collapse is obstruction that dramatically worsens on inhalation. The Cottle maneuver — gently pulling the cheek laterally to open the nasal valve — provides immediate temporary relief and confirms nasal valve collapse as the cause of obstruction. If this simple test opens your airway, nasal valve repair may significantly improve your breathing.

Post-Rhinoplasty Nasal Obstruction

Prior rhinoplasty is one of the most common causes of nasal valve collapse. Cartilage removed to narrow or refine the nose may have been providing critical structural support. When rhinoplasty weakens the nasal valve, spreader grafts and alar batten grafts can restore support and open the airway without significantly changing the cosmetic result of the prior surgery.

Request Evaluation

Traumatic or Congenital Nasal Weakness

Nasal trauma, congenitally weak lower lateral cartilages, or age-related loss of cartilage strength can all cause nasal valve collapse even without prior surgery. Patients with thin, narrow noses or those who have experienced nasal fractures are at increased risk and may benefit from structural reinforcement of the nasal valve.

Proven Surgical Solutions

Techniques With Decades of Clinical Evidence

Spreader grafts — cartilage grafts placed between the upper lateral cartilages and the septum — have been the gold standard for internal nasal valve repair for over 30 years. Alar batten grafts reinforce the external valve against collapse. Both techniques have well-established outcomes and are performed by our board-certified ENT surgeons.

The LATERA absorbable nasal implant offers an alternative for patients who prefer an in-office option. LATERA supports the upper and lower lateral cartilages and gradually absorbs over 18 months, with the nasal tissue maintaining the support gained.

Functional vs. Cosmetic

Restore Breathing — Not Appearance

Nasal valve repair performed purely for functional improvement does not change the outward appearance of your nose. The surgical work is done through internal incisions, and the goal is structural reinforcement rather than reshaping.

For patients who also have cosmetic concerns, nasal valve repair can be combined with rhinoplasty in a single procedure. Our surgeons can address both functional airway issues and aesthetic goals simultaneously, which is often more efficient and reduces overall recovery time compared to staged procedures.

Nasal Valve Repair FAQ

The Cottle maneuver is a simple self-test: place your fingertips on your cheeks beside your nose and gently pull the cheek laterally (outward) while breathing in. If this dramatically improves your breathing, nasal valve collapse is likely contributing to your obstruction. An ENT evaluation with nasal endoscopy will confirm the diagnosis and identify which type of valve collapse is present.

The internal nasal valve is the angle between the upper lateral cartilage and the septum — the narrowest part of the nasal airway. The external nasal valve involves the alar rim and lower lateral cartilage — the nostril opening. Many patients have both. Spreader grafts primarily address internal valve collapse, while alar batten grafts reinforce the external valve. Your surgeon will assess which area requires repair.

Functional nasal valve repair performed to improve breathing is generally covered by most major insurance plans when medical necessity criteria are met. Cosmetic rhinoplasty combined with functional repair may have costs divided between covered and non-covered portions. Our team will verify your benefits and provide a detailed cost estimate prior to scheduling.

Most patients take 1–2 weeks off from work after nasal valve repair. Swelling inside the nose is expected for several weeks, so the full benefit to breathing may not be apparent until 4–6 weeks post-operatively. Strenuous activity is restricted for 2–4 weeks. When performed as a purely internal procedure, there is typically no visible bruising or external swelling.

Yes. Functional nasal valve repair and cosmetic rhinoplasty are frequently performed together. Combining the procedures reduces overall recovery time and anesthetic exposure compared to staging them separately. The functional component is typically covered by insurance, while the cosmetic portion is a patient-pay expense. Your surgeon will provide a detailed breakdown during your consultation.
