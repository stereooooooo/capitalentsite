Visit Preparation

Before Your Appointment

A little preparation goes a long way. Here's what to expect and how to get ready for your visit.

What to Bring

  • Photo ID (driver's license or government-issued ID)
  • Insurance card(s) — primary and secondary if applicable
  • Referral paperwork (if required by your plan)
  • List of current medications with dosages
  • Any prior imaging: CT scans, MRI, or X-ray on disc or digital copy
  • Completed new patient forms (available on the patient portal)
  • Co-pay payment — we accept cash, check, and major credit cards

What to Expect

  • Arrive 15 minutes early to complete any remaining paperwork
  • Your provider will review your health history and chief complaint
  • A thorough examination of your ears, nose, or throat as relevant
  • Possible in-office tests (hearing test, nasal endoscopy, etc.)
  • Discussion of your diagnosis and treatment options
  • Next steps outlined: prescription, referral, imaging, or procedure scheduling
  • Plan for 45–60 minutes for a new patient visit

What to Bring

  • Insurance card (if your plan or coverage has changed)
  • Any new medications added since your last visit
  • New imaging or test results ordered by another provider
  • A written list of questions or concerns for your provider
  • Your co-pay payment

What to Expect

  • Your provider will review how you have responded to treatment
  • Follow-up examination of the relevant area
  • Adjustment of your treatment plan if needed
  • Plan for 20–30 minutes for a follow-up appointment
  • Prescription renewals or referrals as appropriate

Before Allergy Testing

  • Stop antihistamines 5–7 days before testing — including OTC products like Zyrtec, Claritin, Allegra, Benadryl, and Xyzal
  • Stop nasal antihistamine sprays (e.g., Astelin, Patanase) for 5 days before
  • Tricyclic antidepressants (Elavil, Pamelor) must be stopped 7–10 days before — check with your prescriber before stopping
  • Do NOT stop inhaled steroids, nasal steroid sprays, or asthma medications
  • Wear loose, short-sleeved clothing — testing is done on the arms or back
  • Eat normally before your appointment — do not fast

What to Expect

  • Small amounts of allergen extracts are applied to the skin by gentle prick — not injections
  • You wait 15–20 minutes for results to develop
  • A small raised bump at any site indicates allergy to that substance
  • Results are reviewed with you immediately after testing
  • Your allergist will use results to build a customized immunotherapy plan
  • Mild itching and redness at test sites is normal and resolves quickly
  • Plan for 1.5–2 hours for a full allergy evaluation and testing session

Before Your Hearing Evaluation

  • No special preparation is required for a standard hearing test
  • Avoid loud noise exposure (concerts, power tools) for 24 hours before testing if possible
  • Let us know if you have ear canal issues, a perforated eardrum, or active ear infection
  • Bring a list of any medications you take — some drugs (ototoxic medications) can affect hearing
  • For children: bring a comfort item and arrive well-rested for the most accurate results

What to Expect

  • Pure-tone audiometry: you respond to tones played through headphones at different pitches and volumes
  • Speech audiometry: you repeat words to test comprehension
  • Tympanometry: a small device checks eardrum movement and middle ear pressure
  • Painless and non-invasive — no injections or discomfort
  • Results reviewed with you immediately by our audiologist
  • Hearing aid recommendations made if appropriate, with no-pressure guidance
  • Plan for 30–45 minutes for a complete evaluation

Preparing for Your Procedure

  • Follow any medication instructions provided — some blood thinners may need to be stopped
  • Do not take aspirin or ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) for 7–10 days unless your provider approves
  • Arrange a driver if sedation or IV anesthesia is used — do not drive after sedation
  • Wear comfortable, loose clothing — avoid jewelry around the neck or ears
  • Bring your insurance card and photo ID
  • If fasting is required, your care team will give specific instructions in advance
  • Ask any remaining questions before the day of the procedure

What to Expect

  • Local anesthesia will be applied to numb the area before the procedure
  • Your provider will explain each step before they begin
  • Most in-office procedures take 30–60 minutes total
  • You will rest briefly after the procedure before being released
  • Written after-care instructions will be provided before you leave
  • A follow-up appointment is typically scheduled at 2–4 weeks
  • Contact us immediately if you experience severe pain, high fever, or significant bleeding
After Your Procedure

Post-Procedure Care Instructions

Proper aftercare helps you heal faster and get the best possible results. Follow the guidance below for your specific procedure.

Balloon Sinuplasty / Sinus Surgery

Following your sinus procedure, careful aftercare will ensure the best healing and symptom relief.

  • Rinse nasal passages with saline (e.g., NeilMed) 2–3 times daily starting the day after the procedure
  • Avoid blowing your nose forcefully for 1 week
  • Do not swim, use a hot tub, or submerge your face in water for 2 weeks
  • Avoid strenuous exercise or heavy lifting for 1–2 weeks
  • Sleep with your head elevated (30–45°) for the first few nights
  • Mild bloody or discolored nasal discharge is normal for 1–2 weeks
  • Take prescribed medications exactly as directed; do not stop early
  • Call us if you develop a fever over 101°F, severe pain, or heavy bleeding

Tonsillectomy & Adenoidectomy (T&A)

Recovery from tonsil surgery takes 7–14 days. Pain management and hydration are the top priorities.

  • Pain is typically worst on days 3–7 — take pain medication on schedule, not just when pain spikes
  • Drink plenty of cold fluids: water, clear broth, popsicles, smoothies
  • Eat soft, cool foods (ice cream, yogurt, mashed potatoes) — avoid hot, spicy, or crunchy foods for 2 weeks
  • White or yellow scabs forming in the throat are normal — do not try to remove them
  • Avoid strenuous activity and school/work for 7–10 days (adults may need longer)
  • Throat pain may temporarily worsen between days 5–10 as scabs begin to loosen — this is normal
  • Go to the emergency room immediately if you experience active bleeding from the throat

Ear Tube Placement (Tympanostomy)

Ear tube surgery is quick and most children resume normal activity the same day or next day.

  • Most children are back to normal within 24 hours after the procedure
  • A small amount of bloody discharge from the ear immediately after surgery is normal
  • Keep ears dry — use ear plugs or a cotton ball coated in Vaseline for baths and swimming (ask your provider about swimming)
  • Ear drops may be prescribed — use as directed
  • New or increased drainage from the ear after the first week may indicate infection — call us
  • Tubes typically fall out on their own within 6–18 months; follow-up visits monitor this

Septoplasty / Nasal Surgery

Septoplasty and turbinate reduction typically have a 1–2 week recovery period.

  • Do not blow your nose for the first 2 weeks
  • Sneeze with your mouth open to reduce nasal pressure
  • Sleep with your head elevated on 2 pillows for the first week
  • Avoid aspirin and ibuprofen — use acetaminophen (Tylenol) for pain as directed
  • Saline rinses may be recommended starting 24–48 hours after surgery
  • Swelling and congestion may temporarily worsen before improving — this is expected
  • Avoid strenuous activity, heavy lifting, and contact sports for 3–4 weeks
  • Call us if you develop heavy bleeding, high fever, or severe worsening pain

These are general guidelines. Always follow the specific written instructions provided by your surgeon. If you have questions about your recovery, call us at 512-339-4040.

Our physicians contribute to an online library of patient education articles covering common ENT topics. Browse conditions, procedures, and expert advice written specifically for patients. Visit Ask the ENT →
The AAO-HNS is the leading professional organization for ENT specialists. Their patient health information library covers a wide range of ENT conditions in plain language. Visit ENThealth.org →
Part of the National Institutes of Health, the NIDCD provides comprehensive, evidence-based information on hearing loss, balance disorders, tinnitus, voice, speech, and language conditions. Visit NIDCD.nih.gov →
The AAAAI offers reliable patient resources on allergic rhinitis, cedar fever, asthma, food allergies, and immunotherapy options. Great for Austin patients managing year-round allergens. Visit AAAAI.org →
A patient-focused nonprofit providing information on sleep apnea symptoms, diagnosis, CPAP therapy, and alternative treatments. Includes a community forum and support resources. Visit SleepApnea.org →
