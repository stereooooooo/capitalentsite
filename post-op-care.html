Important

When to Seek Immediate Care

Most recoveries are smooth — but know the signs that require prompt attention.

Go to the ER or call 911 immediately if you experience:

  • Active bleeding that does not stop
  • Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
  • Fever above 101.5°F (38.6°C)
  • Severe uncontrolled pain not relieved by medication
  • Signs of dehydration (no urination >8 hours, dry mouth)
  • Swallowing difficulty causing inability to keep fluids down
  • Chest pain or rapid/irregular heartbeat
  • Signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, or pus

Post-Op Guides

Post-Op Care Instruction Sheets

View your post-operative care instructions online or download the PDF to print. Always refer to your specific discharge instructions provided by your physician.

Medications: Take all prescribed medications as directed. Do not stop antibiotics early. Avoid aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, and other NSAIDs unless specifically prescribed — these increase bleeding risk. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is usually safe for pain unless otherwise instructed.

Diet & hydration: Stay well hydrated. Clear fluids, broths, and light meals are typically tolerated first. Avoid alcohol for 24 hours after anesthesia and while taking prescription pain medications.

Driving & alertness: Do not drive for 24 hours after general anesthesia or while taking narcotic pain medication. Arrange transportation in advance.

Follow-up: Attend all scheduled post-operative appointments. These visits are not optional — they are a critical part of your recovery and surgical outcome.

Signs of infection: Increasing (rather than decreasing) redness, warmth, swelling, or pus at any surgical site warrants prompt evaluation. Call our office during business hours, or go to an urgent care or ER after hours.

Throat & Airway Procedures

  • Tonsillectomy Diet, activity, pain management, and when to call after tonsil removal.
    Download PDF

    Diet: Start with cold, soft foods and liquids. Popsicles, ice cream, yogurt, pudding, and cool broths are ideal for the first several days. Avoid hard, scratchy, or spicy foods for at least 2 weeks. Stay well hydrated — dehydration is the most common cause of complications.

    Pain management: Take prescribed pain medication on schedule — do not wait until pain becomes severe. Alternating acetaminophen and ibuprofen (if approved by your physician) is often recommended. Avoid aspirin, which increases bleeding risk.

    Activity: Rest for the first 2–3 days. Avoid strenuous activity, running, or lifting for at least 10–14 days. Children may return to school when they are eating and drinking normally and off narcotic pain medication.

    What to expect: A white or yellow coating on the back of the throat is normal and is part of normal healing — it is not an infection. Ear pain is also common and is referred pain from the throat. Mild bleeding on days 5–10 is possible as the scabs dissolve.

    Call us if: Bright red bleeding from the mouth or nose, fever above 101.5°F, severe dehydration, or inability to swallow any fluids.

Nose & Sinus Procedures

  • Sinus Surgery Nasal rinses, follow-up debridements, and activity guidance after FESS or sinus surgery.
    Download PDF

    Nasal rinses: Begin saline nasal rinses (e.g., NeilMed or neti pot) as directed — usually starting 24–48 hours after surgery. Rinses are critical for removing crusting and blood clots and promoting healing. Rinse gently; do not forcefully blow your nose.

    Medications: Take all prescribed antibiotics, steroids, and nasal sprays as directed. Do not stop them early without physician guidance.

    Activity: Avoid strenuous exercise, heavy lifting, and bending over for at least 1–2 weeks. Avoid flying for 1 week. Sleep with your head elevated 30–45 degrees for the first few nights to reduce swelling.

    What to expect: Significant congestion, nasal drainage (including bloody discharge), and reduced smell for 1–2 weeks is normal. Swelling peaks at 48–72 hours and then gradually improves. Full results may take 4–6 weeks as healing continues.

    Follow-up debridements: Post-op cleaning appointments are an essential part of sinus surgery recovery. Do not miss these visits — crusting that is not removed can compromise your surgical result.

    Call us if: Heavy bright-red bleeding, severe headache, fever above 101.5°F, vision changes, or stiff neck.

Ear & Hearing Procedures

  • Ear Surgery Ear protection, activity restrictions, and healing timeline after general ear surgery.
    Download PDF

    Water precautions: Keep water completely out of the ear until cleared by your surgeon. Use petroleum jelly–coated cotton or custom ear plugs for showering. No swimming until cleared — typically 4–6 weeks for most procedures.

    Ear drops: If prescribed antibiotic ear drops, instill them as directed. Lie with the treated ear facing up for 5 minutes after instillation to ensure drops reach the ear canal.

    Activity: Avoid straining, heavy lifting, and nose blowing for at least 1 week. Avoid flying for 1–2 weeks after most ear surgeries unless specifically cleared. No contact sports until fully healed.

    What to expect: Some drainage (possibly blood-tinged) from the ear canal is normal for the first few days. Mild hearing changes, fullness, and crackling sounds are expected. Hearing typically improves as swelling resolves.

    Call us if: Increasing ear pain after 48 hours, foul-smelling drainage, sudden loss of hearing, facial weakness, severe dizziness, or fever above 101.5°F.

Allergy Procedures

  • Allergy Testing & Treatment Shot schedules, reaction monitoring, and what to do between visits for allergy immunotherapy.
    Download PDF

    After each shot: Wait in the office for the required observation period (typically 20–30 minutes) after every injection. Do not skip this waiting period — most serious reactions occur within this window.

    Local reactions: A small, itchy bump at the injection site is normal and expected. Apply a cold pack and take an antihistamine if itching is bothersome. Do not scratch or rub the site.

    Systemic reactions: Symptoms such as widespread hives, throat tightening, wheezing, dizziness, or a feeling of impending doom require immediate treatment with epinephrine. Use your EpiPen and call 911 immediately. Then call our office.

    Schedule: Consistency is key to the effectiveness of immunotherapy. Missed or delayed shots may require a dosage adjustment. Always tell our staff if you have had any reactions since your last visit.

    EpiPen: Patients on immunotherapy should carry an epinephrine auto-injector at all times as directed by your physician. Make sure it is not expired and that you (and a family member) know how to use it.
